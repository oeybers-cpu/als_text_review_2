Introduction Text 1 Review

Gee (2014)

Gee (2014) introduces the fundamental distinction between "Big 'D' Discourse" and "little 'd' discourse" to capture the complex ways in which people enact and recognize socially and historically significant identities through integrated combinations of language, actions, interactions, objects, tools, technologies, beliefs, and values. This theoretical framework emphasizes that discourse is not merely about language use, but represents a "conversation" among different historically formed social groups and their ways of being.

The notion of "Big 'D' Discourse" sets a larger context for analyzing language in use, recognizing that when we speak or write, we simultaneously accomplish three things: saying something (inform), doing something (act), and being something (be). This tripartite function of language demonstrates that understanding communication requires knowledge of social practices, genres of activity, and the social identities that speakers attempt to enact.

"Reading the word requires reading the world. To understand what is being said in any deep way we need to know what speakers or writers are trying to do." - Paulo Freire (1995)
Social Languages
Different varieties of language used in specific contexts - from gang language to academic discourse, each requiring appropriate "ways with words" to be recognized as a legitimate member.
Primary Discourse
The initial culturally distinctive way of being an "everyday person" acquired early in life within one's primary socializing unit, forming the foundation of identity and vernacular language.
Secondary Discourses
Discourses acquired later in life within public institutions - schools, churches, businesses, governments - each requiring specific ways with words, things, and deeds.
Kinds of People
Discourses are fundamentally about being recognized as certain "kinds of people" - identities that appear and disappear throughout history and require complex performances to "pull off."

Gee's framework reveals how identity construction involves more than just linguistic competence. To successfully enact a social identity - whether as a gang member, honor student, or hip-hop fan - individuals must integrate appropriate language use with gestures, dress, actions, interactions, tools, and values. This complex performance must be recognized and accepted by others within specific contexts, making identity a fundamentally social and contestable phenomenon.

The relationship between primary and secondary Discourses is particularly significant for understanding educational and social mobility. Some social groups achieve alignment with valued secondary Discourses by incorporating aspects of these practices into their children's primary socialization. For example, families affiliated with formal schooling often encourage dinner-time storytelling that resembles academic essays or engage in book-reading practices that mirror school-based questioning and analysis.

The theoretical implications of this framework extend beyond individual identity construction to broader questions of social reproduction and change. Discourses can mix, hybridize, or come into conflict with one another. An African-American politician speaking in a church context might simultaneously draw from both religious and political Discourses, creating complex negotiations of identity that may be interpreted differently by various audiences.

This perspective emphasizes that Discourses are not static categories but dynamic social formations that "struggle" with each other to make society and history. They can change, blend, or disappear as social conditions evolve, highlighting the historical contingency of what counts as recognizable social identities and the ongoing negotiation required to maintain or transform social structures.
